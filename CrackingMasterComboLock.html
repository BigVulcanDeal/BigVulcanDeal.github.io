I spent a few hours today cracking a Master combo lock that was sitting around.  A quick check on Amazon suggests that I was working for well under $1/hour, but the feeling of success made it feel like a lot more.

There are lots of how-to guides out there describing how to whittle potential combinations of a Master combo lock down to 80 possibilities .. or even as few as 8 if you have model with a certain vulnerability.  However, after trying to follow these tips blindly for a little while, I started to suspect that I have a newer model that has been designed to disobey these widely available “how to” guides.  I figured that Master was unlikely to extensively re-engineer a proven design, so I studied up a little on how these things actually work.  A nice overview can be found here, and a nice film can be found here.

Armed with my newfound knowledge of basic Master Combo Lock anatomy, I picked off the "gate" positions for the third wheel by pulling up firmly on shackle and finding where the dial "freezes".  There are 12 such gate positions.  Eleven of these are “false” gates, and one is the "real" gate.  This real gate has two "tells":

  • It's the only "whole number" position with a different last digit than the others (in my case, whole numbers were 0, 10, 20, 30 and 37)
  • It has the widest "range" when locked .. false gates allow you to twist the dial about +/- 1/2 "tick", the real gate allows you to twist it a bit more.  Position 37 was wider than the others, soI now knew that the last digit of the combo was 37.

And at this point, the online guides seemed to go awry, so I had to tap into my rudimentary grasp of lock anatomy.  Most guides claim that the first digit will be mod 4 congruent to the last digit, which would yield candidates 1, 5, ...  37 for the first digit.  This turned out to be incorrect, and here’s how I applied anatomical knowledge of the lock to figure this out.  I turned the clock several times clockwise, and stopped with 0 at the top position .. then I turned it counterclockwise until I felt the third wheel engaging the 2nd wheel (bump) when 38 was at the top.  I kept turning until I felt the 2nd wheel engage the first wheel (bump) when 34 was at the  top.  This suggested that the first digit is congruent to the (third digit + 6) modulo 4 .. yielding candidates 3, 7, 11 ... 39, rather than 1, 5 ... 37.  And I knew that the 2nd digit would be a number that was in the set (1st digit + 2) mod 4 .. so the candidates for the 2nd position were 1, 5, ... 37.   Of course, 37 was not feasible, since it was too close to the known last digit of 37 .. so I was left with as many as 90 combinations to try.

Some of the online guides also claim that you get the first number of the combination directly by feeling when the (superfluous) wheel catch on the back of wheel 1 brushes the shackle retainer.  My lock didn’t seem to have that issue, so I had to just plow through all possible combinations.

Fortunately, the correct combination, 11, 17, 37 was reached pretty quickly, and the lock popped open.
