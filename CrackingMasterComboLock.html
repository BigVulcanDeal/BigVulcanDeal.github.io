I spent a few hours today cracking a Master combo lock that was sitting around.  A quick check on Amazon suggests that I was working for well under $1/hour, but the feeling of success made it feel like a lot more.

There are lots of how-to guides out there describing how to whittle potential combinations of a Master combo lock down to 80 possibilities .. or even as few as 8.  However, after trying to follow these tips blindly for a little while, I started to suspect that I have a newer model that has been designed to disobey these widely available “how to” guides.  I figured that Master was unlikely to extensively re-engineer a proven design, so I studied up a little on how these things actually work.  A nice overview can be found here, and a nice film can be found here.

Armed with my newfound knowledge of basic Master Combo Lock anatomy, I picked off the "gate" positions for the third wheel, which is done by pulling up firmly on shackle and finding where the dial "freezes").  There are 11 false gates, and one "real" gate.  The real gate has two "tells":

  • It's the only "whole number" position with a different last digit than the others (in my case, whole numbers were 0, 10, 20, 30 and 37)
  • It has the widest "range" when locked .. false gates allow you to twist the dial about +/- 1/2 "tick", the real gate allows you to twist it a bit more.

So, I had the last digit of the combo .. 37.

And here’s where the online guides seemed to go awry (and where my rudimentary grasp of lock anatomy paid off).  They claim the first digit will be mod 4 congruent to the last digit (yielding candidates 1, 5, ...  37).  This turned out to be incorrect, and I used anatomical knowledge of the lock to figure this out.  I turned the clock several times clockwise, and stopped with 0 at the top position .. then I turned it counterclockwise until I felt the third wheel engaging the 2nd wheel (bump) when 38 was at the top.  I kept turning until I felt the 2nd wheel engage the first wheel (bump) when 34 was at the  top.  This suggested that the first digit is congruent to the (third digit + 6) modulo 4 .. yielding candidates 3, 7, 11 ... 39, rather than 1, 5 ... 37.  And I knew that the 2nd digit would be a number that was in the set (1st digit + 2) mod 4 .. i.e. 1, 5, ... 37.

A few tries later, and the combination 11, 17, 37 popped the lock open
